The hidden challenge of AI-generated medical chronologies.

The demo is always impressive. An AI tool ingests a few thousand pages of medical records and returns a chronology in minutes — dated, organized, readable. The hidden challenge of AI-generated chronologies isn't getting output. It's knowing what the output is worth. And here is the uncomfortable truth I learned evaluating this workflow inside a firm: if you don't systematize the evaluation, your results are useless.

Useless is a strong word, and I do not use it lightly. Unsystematic testing — different people, on different record sets, with different prompts, judging by impression — produces exactly two outcomes: enthusiasm or distrust. Neither is evidence. The enthusiast saw a clean summary of an easy record set; the skeptic caught one hallucinated date and wrote the technology off. Both are reacting to anecdotes, and a firm that makes adoption decisions on anecdotes is gambling with the document that drives its downstream litigation decisions.

Systematizing the process means three commitments, made before the tool is ever opened. First, break out the roles: who prepares the work product, who reviews it, and whose standard governs what counts as good. A chronology serves attorneys, but its quality is judged at the level of the preparer's expertise — the people who know what a missed nursing entry costs. Second, prequalify the testing parameters: which record set, against what baseline, what counts as an error, and on which dimensions the output will be scored — accuracy, completeness, traceability, attribution, preservation of clinical uncertainty. Decide these in advance, or the results will be reshaped by whatever the output happens to look like. Third, compare changes in both workflow and results: run the same matter the traditional way and the AI-assisted way, and measure the differences entry by entry — not just "was it faster," but "what changed in the work, and what changed in the product."

The spine of all of it is source traceability. Every assertion in a chronology must be tied to a record a reviewer can open. The failure modes that matter most — fabricated detail, critical omissions, misattributed actions, hedged clinical language flattened into false certainty — are invisible to casual reading, because the output is fluent and almost all of it is right. They surface only when output is checked against source, systematically, by someone who knows the records. I've documented these failure modes and the review techniques that catch them in a worked case study.

This discipline is also what lets you see the limits of automation clearly. What we ultimately determined, based on the totality of our qualitative results, was that fully reliable chronology generation was beyond the current scope of the enterprise tools available to us — a verdict we could only reach, and only trust, because the process was rigorous. That conclusion was not a disappointment; it was the product. It told us where human judgment remains primary, gave us a baseline to retest as the technology matures, and protected the firm from misplaced trust in the meantime.

The chronology is where medical-legal work is won and lost, which makes it the worst possible place for impressionistic AI adoption — and the best possible place to learn what disciplined evaluation looks like.
