About

I build the learning that makes complex tools usable

I have spent my career making complex information understandable and turning it into things people can act on — e-learning modules and learning paths, training sessions, quick-reference guides, and knowledge bases. My graduate training in biomedical visualization centered on communicating dense technical material so that non-specialists can understand and use it, and I coordinate curriculum and first-line support for an honors medical-pathway program at the University of Illinois Chicago.

Most recently I have done this work for AI. In 2025 I joined my firm's AI committee and an enterprise AI pilot, integrating Microsoft Copilot and Filevine's Lois into daily workflows. I co-led learning sessions for colleagues from office managers to partners, built and documented workflow guides, evaluated AI output against source records to find where it met or missed the mark, and gathered the feedback that shaped what we rolled out next — the full enablement loop, run on a product that changed under us constantly. This fall I begin the University of Hawaiʻi Executive J.D. program focused on AI law and governance, deepening the domain knowledge behind the work.

Enablement across the full learning lifecycle

How I approach building enablement for a tool that keeps changing — start from the user's real workflow, then build the onboarding, reference content, knowledge base, and feedback signals that keep people confident as the product evolves.

Start from the user's real workflow

Before building a single module, I map how the work actually moves — here, a litigation matter — so enablement targets the moments that matter, not a generic tour of features.

  1. Intake and matter setup
  2. Medical records collection
  3. Chronology and issue spotting
  4. Expert review and preparation
  5. Depositions and motion support
  6. Trial preparation
