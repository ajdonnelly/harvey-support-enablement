What legal AI companies can learn from instructional design.

Throughout my career, I have been drawn to technologies that expand human capability and improve the communication of complex information. My graduate training is in biomedical visualization — a field whose entire purpose is making complex information learnable by a specific audience. My thesis research asked how jurors comprehend expert medical testimony, and whether haptic, self-guided, three-dimensional printed models could improve that comprehension while reducing cognitive burden. I mention this because, after helping bring about AI adoption inside a law firm, I am convinced that legal AI companies are sitting on a problem my field has studied for decades: adoption is a learning problem, and learning problems have a design discipline.

Instructional design starts where most product enablement skips: audience analysis. A juror, a surgeon, and an attorney need different things from the same anatomical model — and a partner, an associate, a paralegal, and a legal secretary need different things from the same AI tool. They hold different prior knowledge, different anxieties, and different workflows. Enablement built for "the user" reaches none of them; enablement built role by role, the way a good lesson is built for its learners, reaches all of them. This is why fact-finding by role is the first thing I do in any adoption effort.

The second lesson is cognitive load. The instinct of every product team is the comprehensive manual, the hour-long webinar, the everything-launch. Learning theory says the opposite: people acquire skills in small, task-specific units, anchored to work they already do, with immediate opportunity to practice. A two-page quick-reference guide for one real workflow outperforms a hundred-page manual every time. The same principle holds for the tools themselves — every additional choice a user must make before getting value is load that an anxious learner will resolve by closing the tab.

Third: learning is social and spaced, not broadcast and singular. Large organizations learn the way individuals do — through repetition, scaffolding, and watching peers. Short recurring sessions where colleagues demonstrate to colleagues build more fluency than any kickoff. And assessment runs both directions: what learners get wrong tells the teacher what is not landing. For a legal AI company, I believe user struggles are not support tickets — they are the most precise product feedback that exists, if someone is designed into the loop to capture them.

Finally, durable knowledge transfer. Product updates are a curriculum problem disguised as a release-notes problem: a feature announcement tells users what changed; instruction tells them what it means for their Tuesday. The companies that translate every release into workflow impact — and that help firms build internal knowledge bases where one person's discovery becomes everyone's practice — are doing instructional design whether they call it that or not. I've built this kind of material in Articulate Rise and coordinated curriculum for a medical scholars program, and the principles transfer exactly.

Legal AI companies are competing on model capability today. I believe they will be competing on learning velocity soon, because firms can only adopt as fast as their people learn. The companies that staff and design for that — the way educators would — will out-adopt the ones that ship features and documentation and hope for the best.
