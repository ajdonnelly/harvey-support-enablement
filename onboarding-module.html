Week 1, Module 3: how Harvey grounds answers

A self-guided micro-module for a brand-new support specialist. By the end, a new hire understands where Harvey’s answers come from, why citations matter, the one habit — verify before you act — that underpins almost every good support interaction, and how to answer the data-security questions users ask most.

About this sample

Independent work sample built only from Harvey’s public product information; not affiliated with or endorsed by Harvey, and free of any confidential material. It’s written to show how I structure a learning object — objectives, content, a check, and the design reasoning — not to be a literal Harvey course.

AudienceNew support specialist, day 2–3
Time~15 minutes, self-paced

Objectives

By the end of this module, you can…

  1. Name the sources Harvey can draw on when it answers a question.
  2. Explain to a user, in one sentence, what a citation is for and why it matters.
  3. Demonstrate the “verify before you act” habit by expanding a citation and confirming a claim against its source.
  4. Recognize a source-scope issue — the most common reason an answer “misses” something — and know it’s usually not a bug.
  5. Answer the common data-security questions — does Harvey train on our data, where is it stored, who can see it — and route detailed ones to the right place.

Concept 1

Harvey answers from sources, not from thin air

The single most important idea for supporting this product: Harvey is built to ground its answers in sources a user can check. Depending on how a query is set up, those sources can include the user’s own uploads, documents stored in a Vault, content synced from a document management system (like iManage, SharePoint, or Google Drive), premium legal databases, curated public sources, and web search. For harder questions, Harvey can run an agentic search — writing and refining its own searches over those sources until it has enough to answer.

The practical takeaway for support: Harvey can only cite what it was given. When an answer seems to “ignore” something, your first instinct should be to check what was actually in scope — not to assume the model failed.

Concept 2

Citations are the trust mechanism

Every grounded answer comes with citations so a user can verify accuracy before they share or act on it. That’s not a nice-to-have — for a legal professional, the ability to trace a claim back to its source is the difference between a draft they can use and one they can’t. When you help a user, you’re very often really helping them verify: open the citation, read the cited passage, confirm it supports the claim.

The one habit to model every time

Verify before you act. Expand the citation → read the source → confirm the claim. If a user adopts only one behavior from their first week with Harvey, this is the one — and modeling it is the highest-leverage thing you do on a ticket.

Concept 3

Where your information goes: security & data control

Supporting Harvey means handling trust at two levels: is this answer right? (citations) and is our data safe? (security). For legal clients, the second is often the first question they ask. You don’t need to be a security engineer — but you should be able to give accurate, calm answers to the common questions, and know when to route the rest.

  1. No model training. Harvey contractually guarantees it does not use your inputs, outputs, or uploaded documents to train the underlying models.
  2. Data sovereignty & control. Customers choose what to upload, set retention policies, can delete data at any time, and keep data in-region (EU/Switzerland, US, or Australia).
  3. Access controls. Enterprise controls like SAML SSO, audit logs, and IP allow-listing; inside the product, permissions govern who can access, view, and edit content.
  4. Independently validated. SOC 2 Type II, ISO 27001 / 27701 / 42001, GDPR, and CCPA, plus third-party security audits.
Know the headlines; route the details

You should be able to confidently answer the common questions above. For specific contractual, compliance, or configuration questions, point the user to the security/trust portal and loop in the right internal team rather than improvising. A security answer that’s subtly wrong erodes the exact trust you’re there to protect — when unsure, route, don’t guess.

Knowledge check

Four quick questions

Answer before expanding each one. These mirror real first-week tickets.

Q1 A user says Harvey “ignored” a key contract. First move?

Answer: Check the source scope — was that contract actually in the query’s sources (upload / Vault / synced DMS)? Most “ignored document” reports are source-scope issues, not model errors.

Q2 A user asks, “Why should I trust this answer?” One sentence?

Answer: “Each claim is cited to a source you can open and check yourself — expand the citation and confirm it before you rely on it.”

Q3 True or false: if a claim has no citation, treat it as verified because Harvey produced it.

Answer: False. A claim with no citation should be treated as unverified until confirmed against a source. Coach the user to check, and flag it if it repeats.

Q4 A user asks, “Does Harvey use our uploaded documents to train its AI models?”

Answer: No — Harvey contractually guarantees it doesn’t train on your inputs, outputs, or uploads. Give that headline confidently; for contractual specifics, point the user to the security/trust portal. Never guess on security.

For the enablement team

Why this module is built this way

A short note on the instructional-design choices — the kind of rationale I’d include for whoever maintains the content.

01

One concept, early

“Grounding and citations” is taught in Week 1 because it underlies most later tickets. Teaching it first makes everything downstream cheaper.

02

Objectives are observable

Each objective is something a new hire can do (name, explain, demonstrate, recognize) — so the check can actually measure it.

03

Check mirrors real tickets

The four questions are scenarios, not trivia — including the security question users ask early. Reinforcement works when it rehearses the actual job.

04

One habit, repeated

“Verify before you act” recurs in the concept, the callout, and the check. Spaced repetition of a single behavior beats a list of ten tips.
