The Legal AI Pilot Playbook

A 90-day framework for introducing AI into law firm operations without treating it as a software rollout. This playbook generalizes what I learned helping build an enterprise AI pilot inside a litigation firm: serving on the firm's AI committee as the voice of the paralegal function, I developed the paralegal portion of the pilot — defining the demo scenarios, pain points, and use cases that drove the vendor's sandbox design — working with staff across six offices, internal technology stakeholders, firm administrators, and the vendor's change-management team.

A note on provenance

This framework is generalized from practice. It names no firms, vendors, or internal documents — the structure is the lesson, and it is designed to be adapted to any practice group or tool stack.

Step 1

Level-set before anyone logs in

Before the pilot tool is ever opened, bring every stakeholder — partners, associates, paralegals, secretaries, and administrators — to a shared baseline on what these systems are, how they work, and what they can and cannot do. This step is not optional preamble: without it, every step that follows evaluates impressions instead of the product. People cannot meaningfully judge a technology they do not understand.

01

What the technology is

Language models generate output; they do not retrieve it from a database of facts. That single distinction explains most of their power and most of their failure modes — and most stakeholder misconceptions. Clarifying this early contextualizes hallucination, omission, and the other errors that are significant concerns for every legal team.

02

What it can and cannot do

Current strengths: synthesis, summarization, drafting, finding patterns across volume. Current limits: fabricated detail, inconsistency between runs, and degraded performance on tasks that exceed what the system can attend to at once. These constraints, once understood, help stakeholders see why pilot scope must be limited and workflow analysis focused.

03

The stakeholder's real job

No matter your position in the firm, you are an expert in your space. The most important role one can play in a pilot is unlocking the knowledge in their own expertise: articulating what good work product looks like, where the pain lives, and what a tool must get right to matter. Learning only how partners use a product will not lead to firm-wide success.

04

The evaluation mindset

The question should never be "is this impressive?" Rather, it should be "does this meet a specific core need my expertise defines — and how would I know?" That framing turns every user into an evaluator from day one. Feedback should be detailed, task-specific, and critical — directly responsive to the workflows and use cases under test, and scoped tightly enough to stay meaningful.

Step 2

Govern before you pilot

The industry suggests that many AI pilots fail before day one: they launch as tool trials instead of governed experiments and closely managed engagements with the human layer. Six principles, agreed in writing before anyone opens the tool, change that.

01

Few workflows, chosen by role

Explicitly select a small set of common workflow items specific to each expert position — a handful per role, no more. Breadth dilutes findings; a tight, role-specific selection produces evidence.

02

Low-risk scope

Pilot in support workflows only. No final legal analysis, no client-facing conclusions, no unreviewed filings — ever.

03

Human review, always

Every AI output is reviewed by qualified staff before use. "Zero unreviewed outputs" is a rule, not a goal.

04

Measured against baseline

Capture a sense of how workflows work today in practice, how long they take to complete, and what level of quality is being achieved before the pilot starts. A claim of time-saving or quality enhancement without a baseline is an anecdote.

05

Secure by default

Only firm-approved tools touch matter data. The pilot defines the approved list and the consequences for going around it.

06

Owned and iterative

One named owner runs the pilot, triages issues, and revises guidelines as findings come in. Pilots without an owner drift; pilots without iteration calcify.

Step 3

Draw the scope line in writing

The single most trust-building artifact in a pilot is an explicit included/excluded list. From my experience, attorneys relax when they can see what the AI will never touch. Limiting scope this way early on also simplifies data permissions, minimizes data leakage, and helps calm privacy concerns for managers.

Included

Where AI earns its keep safely.

  1. Summarization of case materials for internal use
  2. Draft formatting and document organization
  3. First-pass drafting assistance
  4. Administrative and scheduling workflow support
  5. Building practical AI fluency through daily use

Excluded

The bright lines that make the pilot safe to say yes to.

  1. Final legal analysis or conclusions
  2. Independent legal judgment of any kind
  3. Filings or submissions without full human review
  4. Sensitive data in unapproved systems
  5. Autonomous actions without a human decision

Step 4

Inventory the work — and ask three questions of every workflow

Before any tool is evaluated, map what each role actually does — by office, not by job title. The same title can mean different work in different offices, so fact-finding has to happen role by role, location by location. We found this to be especially true in our firm, where staff at one level carried a slightly different basket of tasks than the same role in another office — in a larger law firm network, never assume different nodes work the same way. Then hold every candidate workflow up to three questions.

1. Client value

Does the change let us offer the client something better — faster answers, lower cost, fewer omissions? A workflow improvement that never reaches the client is overhead reduction, not transformation.

2. Firm workflow

How does the change move work through the firm — what gets reordered, what gets reviewed differently, where does the saved time actually go? Reliability is the gating question: speed that can't be trusted changes nothing.

3. Employee burden

Does it improve the working lives of the people doing the work? This is the most overlooked question in legal AI evaluation — and often where the clearest, most immediate gains live.

A worked example: the litigation paralegal workflow inventory

The inventory below reflects an idealized basket of workflows characteristic of the paralegal function in a medical malpractice defense practice, with the obvious pain point each workflow carries — the starting point for deciding where AI evaluation effort should go first. This thought experiment — unpacking and defining the workflow items within an expert's purview — can be done in any firm, for any role; this is one generic perspective, based on what we found in our practice. It gives a sense of the thoughtfulness and granularity required before a single program is opened. The added benefit of this approach is that it gets experts using the technology on low-stakes items in their own workflows — it is as much about getting them involved as getting results.

Where to start — and what we found

Chronologies earned the first evaluation slot in our pilot: they are the most common and time-intensive paralegal task, the basis for litigation decisions downstream, and — in my testing — among the hardest tasks for general-purpose enterprise tools to do well. They are also where all three questions converge, and the pilot bore that out in both directions. The AI-assisted first pass showed real promise: dramatically faster work, a less depleting reviewer posture than five unassisted days in a fifteen-thousand-page record, and clear potential — under structured human review — for fewer omissions and more tailored work product. But our needs were running ahead of what the wrapped AI tool we were testing could offer: at least in the way we were using it, the output was not yet reliable enough for full production use. That finding was as valuable as any gain. It gave firm decision-makers an honest picture of present capability rather than a sales pitch, a documented baseline to retest as the technology matures, and a precise map of where human review effort must concentrate in the meantime.

Two cautions from practice. First, understand the limitations of your tools in the present — and do not assume they will stay limited. We expect progress to make these workflows more and more useful, and our baseline means we will be able to measure that progress when it arrives. Second, client work product belongs only in firm-approved environments, and the approved environment is not always the strongest performer at a given task. Many enterprise tools are wrappers around frontier models: expect the wrappers to improve, but likely on a lag behind the frontier releases they are built on — have patience.

Step 5

Run it in three 30-day phases

What we found is that ninety days is long enough to produce real findings and short enough to keep leadership's attention. Each phase has a distinct job.

Days 1–30: Explore

Finalize approved tools and written guidelines. Identify high-value workflows. Test low-risk use cases. Capture baseline metrics — this is the phase teams skip, and the reason their day-90 report has nothing to show.

Days 31–60: Integrate

Move AI into daily operations on the included workflows. Refine prompts and document what works as reusable patterns. Run weekly office hours — adoption spreads through colleagues watching colleagues, not through memos.

Days 61–90: Decide

Analyze impact against baseline. Gather structured user feedback. Document best practices into a guidance library. Present leadership a decision, not a summary: expand, extend, or stop — against criteria set on day one.

Step 6

Measure three things, not one

Efficiency alone is a vanity metric. A pilot's real output is a known risk profile and a team that trusts the workflow. Track all three families — and set targets up front.

FamilyMetricsWhat "good" looks like
Operational Time saved per workflow vs. baseline; reduction in repetitive tasks; turnaround speed Sustained gains on at least two included workflows, not one-off wins
Adoption Frequency of use; user comfort and confidence; workflows integrated into daily practice Usage that grows without prompting after week six
Risk & quality Error rates observed; hallucination incidents logged; compliance with review procedures Every incident logged and triaged; zero unreviewed outputs in use

Step 7

Lessons from practice

The framework above produced helpful results for us — but it is by no means the only approach. These are the lessons running one exposed: the mistakes to avoid and the additions that turn a good pilot into a durable adoption program.

+1 Ground the pilot in institutional knowledge — and the client's voice

The most common pilot mistake is evaluating a tool against generic demo data instead of the firm's reality. A sandbox should be built from institutional knowledge: real matter types, documented pain points, and simulated working conditions defined by the people who actually do the work. And law firms serve clients — for a defense practice, institutional clients like health systems and insurers have firm expectations about how their matters are handled. Their perspective, and where appropriate their direct feedback, belongs in use-case design from the start. When we ran fact-finding by role and built demo scenarios around our clients' realities, the vendor's sandbox stopped being a feature tour and started answering the only question that matters: will this work here?

+2 Decision criteria, set on day one

Define before launch what results trigger "expand," "extend," or "stop." Without pre-committed thresholds, the day-90 readout becomes a vibes meeting, and pilots that should scale stall — or pilots that should stop limp on.

+3 A vendor feedback loop

Pilots generate exactly the structured user insight AI vendors need — logged errors, missed expectations, workflow friction. Routing it to the vendor in regular, specific terms turns a customer relationship into a product partnership, and gets your issues fixed first.

+4 A training cadence, not a kickoff

One launch session does not build fluency. Short recurring learning sessions — real matters, real prompts, colleagues demonstrating to colleagues — are where attorneys move from skepticism to daily use. This cannot be stressed enough: this is the real, hard work of the human layer. It requires going to people's desks, working them through small pressure points, and constantly making yourself accessible. An environment of confidence and momentum has to develop in the culture of the pilot if there is any chance of a successful firm-wide rollout.

+5 The engagement trifecta: you, your colleagues, and the vendor's change managers

An extension of the cadence lesson above: colleague-to-colleague learning is only part of the engine. The trifecta that promotes engagement adds close collaboration with your platform's change managers. Adoption means changing how people think about how workflows happen, and that change has to be facilitated — working closely with change managers in the sandbox to scaffold knowledge deliberately. More knowledge equals more confidence; more confidence equals more usage. Once that loop starts, it percolates out into the firm, and colleague-to-colleague transfer begins happening on its own.

+6 A QC workflow matched to failure modes

"Human review" is only as good as the reviewer's checklist. Train reviewers on the specific ways AI output fails — dates, omissions, attribution, certainty inflation, unsupported assertions — as documented in my chronology evaluation case study.
